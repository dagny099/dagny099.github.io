In [KPMG's new Global AI Pulse](https://kpmg.com/xx/en/our-insights/ai-and-technology/ai-pulse.html), the most telling number isn't one they put in a headline. It's a distance: the gap between deploying AI broadly and proving it pays roughly tripled this quarter — from 5 points to 15\.

Here's what the study measured. The survey is a serious instrument: 2,145 senior leaders across 20 countries, run every quarter, so you can watch the trend lines move. This quarter nearly all of them moved up. **76% say AI is delivering meaningful business value**, *up 12 points in a single quarter*. And on KPMG's AI maturity curve, the share of companies at broad deployment jumped from 13% to 22% — [the largest shift of any stage](https://kpmg.com/xx/en/media/press-releases/2026/06/growing-adoption-signals-progress-as-cost-visibility-and-accountability-drive-ai-value.html) this quarter.

Then there's the last stage on that curve — "established ROI," where the returns are proven. It read 7% this quarter, against 8% last quarter. On a sample of 2,145, a one-point move on a \~7% figure is inside the margin of error, so the honest reading is that it didn't move. While broad deployment surged, the group that can prove returns stayed flat in the single digits — and the gap between the two tripled.

That gap is the story, and it's worth being precise about why. The 9-point adoption jump is real signal; the 1-point ROI tick is within the noise. What's left when you strip that out is a widening distance between companies that have *deployed* AI and companies that can show it *paid off* — the same divide [an MIT study this year](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/) found from the opposite direction, reporting that 95% of enterprise GenAI pilots delivered no measurable P\&L return.

What separates the organizations clearing that final bar? Governance, of the unglamorous kind. Where it's clear who owns an AI decision, KPMG reports established ROI at roughly three times the rate; where leaders can see what their AI costs to run, about five times. Only a third have that cost visibility today. I'd be careful turning those multipliers into a to-do list, though. The kind of company that can name who owns a decision is usually disciplined in a dozen other ways too — in procurement, in data hygiene, in how it evaluates a model before shipping. The multiplier is a symptom of that maturity, not a lever you pull to manufacture it. It's a mirror, not a recipe.

This is where my read diverges from the report's. KPMG files cost visibility under economics, next to budgets and pricing. I'd file it next to model evaluation, because it's the same move: making an invisible property of a system legible enough to act on. You can't improve what you can't measure. Evaluation instruments a model's quality; cost visibility instruments its spend — same instinct, different axis. Seen that way, the nearly half of companies that scaled back or paused a deployment once costs outran value weren't retreating. They were running an evaluation loop. The teams without visibility can't run that loop at all.

Two things to hold while you read it. First, every figure is a senior leader grading their own organization — self-reported, not audited, by the people with the most reason to be optimistic, and the maturity stages are self-placement too. That's still a real signal, as long as you read it as executive sentiment and not proof. Second, US leaders were held to a higher bar than everyone else ($1B+ in revenue vs. $50M+ elsewhere), which quietly shapes any country comparison.

And it's worth clocking who's holding the survey. The report closes with five KPMG offerings that map cleanly onto the three problems it just diagnosed — a study about AI adoption, produced partly with AI (they say so), recommending the firm that wrote it. None of that makes the data wrong. It's a map drawn by someone who also sells the territory, and you read those a little differently.

My own read: this is a governance story before a technology one, and the companies pulling ahead share something dull. They can answer two plain questions about any AI system: who owns this decision, and what does it cost to run?
