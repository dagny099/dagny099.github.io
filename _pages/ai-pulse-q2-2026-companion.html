The findings · the maturity curve

Where the crowd moved

KPMG places each organization on a six-stage curve, from early research to "established ROI." The story of the quarter is a crowd surging into the second-to-last stage — broad deployment — while the final stage, proven returns, stayed put and even ticked down.

Self-reported phase of the AI journey · Q1 → Q2 · the deltas, not the levels, carry the story

The findings · the differentiators

What the leaders had in common

KPMG's sharpest finding is about what separates the organizations clearing that final gate. Two capabilities stand out, and the report attaches large multipliers to both.

1 · Someone is clearly accountable

Three-quarters of leaders say their CEO actively owns AI as a strategic priority. Far fewer can say who is actually accountable for an AI-informed decision — only 24% name the CEO or executive committee, with the rest spread thin across functions and committees. Where accountability is clearly defined, the outcomes diverge sharply.

Strongly agree · YES = CEO clearly accountable, NO = CEO less / not accountable · Q2

2 · Leaders can see what AI costs

Only about a third of organizations have full, actively-monitored visibility into what their AI costs to run. Nearly half have already rephased a deployment — scaled back, delayed, or paused it — once the costs began to outweigh the value. And the organizations that can see their costs report proven returns at five times the rate of those that can't.

A closer look · my read

The 3× and 5× are not levers

Where I'd slow down before turning a finding into a to-do list

Read quickly, those multipliers sound like instructions: appoint an accountable executive, stand up a cost dashboard, collect 3× to 5× more ROI. KPMG is careful to note that accountability alone doesn't create value — and that caution is worth taking seriously, because it points at how these numbers actually work.

Both findings compare organizations that have a discipline against organizations that don't. But the kind of company that can name who owns an AI decision is usually disciplined in a dozen other ways too: in procurement, in data hygiene, in how it evaluates a model before shipping it. The same is true of cost visibility. These aren't isolated switches. They're symptoms of organizational maturity — and maturity is the thing that produces returns.

Toggle between the two readings:

This doesn't make the finding useless. It makes it a mirror rather than a recipe. The right question to ask your own organization isn't "have we appointed someone?" — it's "are we the kind of organization that already knows who owns this, and what it costs?" If the answer is no, the title alone won't manufacture the maturity underneath it.

A closer look · my read

Cost visibility is an evaluation problem

Why this chapter is really about measurement discipline

KPMG files cost visibility under economics, next to budgets and pricing. I'd file it next to model evaluation, because it's the same move: making an invisible property of a system legible enough to act on. You can't improve what you can't measure. Evaluation instruments a model's quality; cost visibility instruments its spend. Same instinct, different axis.

Seen that way, the 49% who rephased a deployment weren't retreating. They were running an evaluation loop — measuring value against cost, then making a keep-or-kill call. The two-thirds without visibility can't run that loop at all. They're flying a system they can't see the instruments for.

The evaluation habitIts cost-visibility twin
Define the metric before you ship→Set a token / usage budget up front
Monitor quality in production→Watch operating cost as it scales
Attribute outcomes to a model→Attribute spend to a use case
Kill the experiment that underperforms→Rephase the deployment that overspends

For an organization in the two-thirds without visibility, the practical takeaway runs ahead of any strategy change: instrument first. Cost dashboards, per-use-case attribution, and budgets are the same rigor a good ML team already brings to its metrics, pointed at the bill. The strategy gets easier once you can see.

Before you cite it · read it yourself

What the survey is, and what it isn't

A good number deserves a little suspicion. None of this undermines the report — it just tells you how much weight each figure can bear.

Who answered
2,145 senior leaders with direct knowledge of AI use
How big
Companies with US$50M+ revenue — but US$1B+ for the US sub-sample
Where
20 countries and territories, spanning five regions
When
Fielded online, 28 April – 25 May 2026
01
Every figure is self-reported.These are leaders grading their own organizations, not audited books or individual contributors. Read the numbers as executive sentiment, which is real and useful, as long as you don't mistake it for proof.
02
"Established ROI" is a soft construct.It's defined as being able to show that outcomes justify the investment — but it's still a self-assessment. When only ~7% clear that bar, the definition is doing a lot of quiet work.
03
The geographic mix is undisclosed.The report names 20 countries but never says how the 2,145 split across them. Combined with the higher US revenue bar, that makes any "the US is different" reading — like the jump in US worker resistance — rest on an unknown, structurally-different base.
04
Consider who's holding the survey.The report closes with five KPMG service offerings that map neatly onto the three problems it diagnoses, and it discloses using AI in its own production. None of that makes the data wrong. It's a map drawn by someone who also sells the territory.
The takeaway · two questions

The test that cuts through it

Strip away the multipliers and the maturity curve, and the organizations pulling ahead share something plain: they can answer two questions about any given AI system. Try them on yours.

Could you name who is accountable for a specific AI-informed decision in your organization?

Could you see what one AI system costs your organization to run this month?

If both answers came easily, the report's headline findings probably already describe you. If they didn't, that's the more useful place to start than any new model.
